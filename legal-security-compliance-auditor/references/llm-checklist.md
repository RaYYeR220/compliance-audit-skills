# LLM / AI Disclosure & Privacy Checklist

This file is loaded by `SKILL.md` during Phase 3 when LLM or AI integrations are detected in Phase 1. It covers transparency to users, privacy of data sent to AI providers, and obligations under GDPR Art. 22 (automated decisions) and the EU AI Act (in force since August 2024, with tiered application).

LLM features intersect several legal regimes. The same finding may carry GDPR, CCPA, AI Act, and FTC implications — note the most relevant in the report.

---

## 1. Disclosure of AI generation to users

**Check**: Users interacting with an AI system or with AI-generated content must be informed of that fact. EU AI Act Art. 50 makes this an explicit obligation for general-purpose chatbots and synthetic content; many regulators expect it under GDPR transparency principles regardless.

**Look for**:
- Chat or assistant UI components rendering LLM output.
- AI-generated images, video, or voice in user-facing surfaces.
- Auto-generated emails, summaries, or recommendations attributed to the platform.

**Red flags**:
- AI-generated assistant or chatbot reply not labeled as AI.
- AI-generated content (writing, images) presented as if produced by a human.
- A "live agent" framing while the conversation is actually AI.

**Severity**: High when AI presents as human in EU/UK scope. Medium otherwise.

**Fix recommendation**: Add a visible label near the AI feature ("This reply is generated by AI") and a one-line description of capabilities and limits. For deepfake / synthetic media features, mark the output (watermark and metadata) and disclose at the point of generation.

---

## 2. Inaccuracy warning

**Check**: Users must understand that AI output can be wrong. A generic "AI can make mistakes — check important info" line near the AI surface is the de facto standard.

**Look for**: Warning text near chat input, on the AI-generated content, or in a tooltip.

**Red flags**: No warning anywhere on the AI surface.

**Severity**: Medium.

**Fix recommendation**: Add a clear, persistent inaccuracy notice. Where the AI gives recommendations that could be relied upon (medical, legal, financial, travel, safety), strengthen the warning and add domain-specific disclaimers (item 8).

---

## 3. Data sent to the LLM provider — minimization

**Check**: User content sent to a third-party LLM provider is a disclosure of personal data to a processor. It must be minimal, lawful, and disclosed.

**Look for**:
- Where prompts are constructed and what user data is included.
- Whether the system prompt or context includes data from other users.
- Whether full user records (with PII not needed for the task) are included in the prompt.

**Red flags**:
- Sending entire `User` rows (with email, phone, address) to the LLM when only the user's first name is needed.
- Cross-user leakage: another user's data ends up in the prompt for the current request.
- Including auth tokens or system secrets in the prompt.

**Severity**: Critical for cross-user leakage or token leakage. High for over-sharing PII.

**Fix recommendation**: Define explicit prompt templates with only the fields needed. Add a pre-flight check that strips disallowed fields. Use placeholders or hashes for IDs you don't need to disclose. Log prompt shape (not content) for audit.

---

## 4. Privacy policy disclosure of AI processors

**Check**: The LLM provider is a processor. They must be disclosed in the privacy policy with category of data shared and purpose.

**Look for**: Mentions of `OpenAI`, `Anthropic`, `Google`, `Mistral`, `Cohere`, `AWS Bedrock`, `Azure OpenAI`, `xAI`, etc. in the privacy policy.

**Red flags**: Active LLM integration, no disclosure.

**Severity**: High.

**Fix recommendation**: Add a "third parties" or "AI providers" section listing each provider, what data is sent, the purpose, the data residency, and a link to the provider's DPA.

---

## 5. Provider training opt-out

**Check**: Most major LLM providers offer enterprise terms or settings that exclude API traffic from being used to train their models. Without these, the default for some products is to use data for training.

**Defaults to verify**:
- OpenAI API: data not used for training by default since March 2023; double-check with the deployment (Assistants API, ChatGPT Enterprise specifics).
- Anthropic API: not used for training.
- Google Gemini API: free tier may use prompts to improve; paid tier does not.
- Azure OpenAI: not used to train OpenAI models.
- Open-source / self-hosted: depends on deployment.

**Look for**:
- Provider client configuration. Custom endpoints (`baseURL`) pointing at a third-party gateway or proxy that may have its own retention.
- Use of free-tier APIs in production.

**Red flags**: Production traffic on a free-tier endpoint that uses prompts for training.

**Severity**: High when PII flows to a free-tier endpoint with training enabled.

**Fix recommendation**: Use paid/business tiers. Sign DPAs. Document the training opt-out status per provider in the privacy policy.

---

## 6. Logging and retention of AI interactions

**Check**: Logs of prompts and completions are often PII-rich. They are themselves subject to all the data-protection rules (right to deletion, retention limits, encryption, access controls).

**Look for**:
- Tables / files capturing prompt and response.
- Whether they are included in the right-to-erasure flow.
- Retention policy.

**Red flags**:
- LLM transcripts retained indefinitely.
- Transcripts not deleted when the user is deleted.
- Transcripts available to anyone in the company without access controls.

**Severity**: High when transcripts contain PII and are not deleted with the user.

**Fix recommendation**: Cap retention (e.g. 30–90 days). Include transcripts in the deletion cascade. Encrypt at rest. Restrict access. Allow users to delete individual conversations.

---

## 7. Output not used as sole basis for consequential decisions

**Check**: GDPR Art. 22 grants the right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effects. EU AI Act classifies many decision-support uses as "high-risk" with extensive obligations.

**Look for**:
- LLM outputs that determine eligibility (loan approval, insurance pricing, hiring shortlist, content moderation that closes accounts).
- Workflows where the LLM's verdict is acted upon without human review.

**Red flags**:
- Auto-close account on LLM-flagged abuse, no appeal.
- LLM as the sole arbiter of a loan or hire decision.
- Automated rejection emails generated and sent without review.

**Severity**: High in EU scope. Medium elsewhere.

**Fix recommendation**: Build a human-in-the-loop step for any consequential decision. Provide a clear appeal path. Document the model used, the inputs, and the safeguards. Re-classify the feature against EU AI Act Annex III when applicable.

---

## 8. Domain-specific disclaimers

**Check**: AI features in regulated or high-risk domains (medical, legal, financial, mental health, safety) need clear disclaimers and limits.

**Look for**:
- Symptom checkers, medication advice, mental health chat, legal document analysis, financial advice, tax computation, travel safety, driving advice, child supervision features.

**Red flags**: Domain-targeted AI feature with only a generic "AI can be wrong" notice.

**Severity**: High.

**Fix recommendation**: Add a domain-specific disclaimer with the standard "not a substitute for professional advice" phrasing tailored to the domain. For medical: encourage contacting a clinician and provide emergency contact info. For financial: clarify the output is not investment advice. For mental health: add crisis hotline links by user country.

---

## 9. Prompt injection and untrusted inputs

**Check**: When the LLM consumes content from untrusted sources (user uploads, scraped pages, third-party emails, RAG documents), prompt injection can hijack the model into following attacker instructions.

**Look for**:
- RAG pipelines pulling from user-uploaded documents or scraped web content.
- Email-handling AI features (assistants that read inboxes).
- Tool-using agents with high-impact tools (send email, transfer money, modify files).

**Red flags**:
- Agent with tools and no allow-list of safe actions.
- No mechanism to detect and refuse instruction-bearing content from untrusted sources.
- Tool outputs fed back into the prompt without sanitation.

**Severity**: High when an agent with consequential tools ingests untrusted content.

**Fix recommendation**: Treat all retrieved content as data, not instructions. Apply strict tool allow-lists. Require human confirmation for high-impact tool calls. Use guardrail models or pattern checks for known injection signatures. Sandbox tool execution.

---

## 10. Output sanitization

**Check**: LLM-generated text rendered as HTML is a fresh XSS vector.

**Look for**: AI-generated content rendered with `dangerouslySetInnerHTML`, `v-html`, raw template interpolation, or sent to a markdown renderer without safe defaults.

**Red flags**: LLM output rendered as raw HTML.

**Severity**: High.

**Fix recommendation**: Render as text or as markdown with a sanitizer that disables raw HTML and `javascript:` URLs. If HTML must be rendered, run through `DOMPurify` or equivalent.

---

## 11. Vector store / embeddings privacy

**Check**: Embeddings of user content stored in a vector DB are still derived from PII and remain personal data. Same rules apply for deletion, encryption, retention.

**Look for**:
- Pinecone, Weaviate, Chroma, Qdrant, pgvector, Milvus, FAISS, lancedb integrations.
- Whether embeddings are deleted when source content is deleted.
- Whether vector records are encrypted at rest.

**Red flags**:
- Deletion deletes source content but not embeddings.
- Vector store hosted in a third country with no DPA.

**Severity**: High when deletion doesn't propagate. Medium when residency is unclear.

**Fix recommendation**: Add embeddings to the deletion cascade. Record `(user_id, source_id)` metadata to enable per-user erasure. Choose providers with EU regions when needed.

---

## 12. Cross-border AI processing

**Check**: Sending PII to a US-hosted LLM provider from EU/UK users is a cross-border transfer. Same rules as GDPR Art. 44–49.

**Look for**:
- Provider regions actually configured (`OPENAI_BASE_URL`, Anthropic region, Azure region).
- Default region of the SDK.

**Red flags**: EU traffic routed through US-only endpoints with no documented SCCs.

**Severity**: High in EU/UK scope.

**Fix recommendation**: Use EU residency offerings where available. Confirm SCCs / DPAs are in place. Disclose in the privacy policy.

---

## 13. Children and AI

**Check**: AI features used by minors have additional duties (UK Age-Appropriate Design Code; COPPA in US; EU AI Act considerations for systems targeting children).

**Look for**: AI feature exposure to under-16 (or under-13 US) users. Age gating before AI features.

**Red flags**: AI chat exposed to minors with no parental consent flow.

**Severity**: High.

**Fix recommendation**: Apply parental-consent flows before exposing AI features to minors. Lock down content filters. Disable retention for minors.

---

## 14. Model card and explanation

**Check**: Users may have a right to receive meaningful information about the logic involved in automated decisions (GDPR Art. 13(2)(f), 15(1)(h), 22(3); EU AI Act for high-risk).

**Look for**: A `MODEL_CARD.md` or equivalent. A disclosure page explaining how the AI feature works at a high level.

**Severity**: Medium for high-risk uses. Low otherwise.

**Fix recommendation**: Publish a short explanation of the AI feature: what data it uses, what the output is, what it is not designed to do, what to do if it makes a mistake.

---

## 15. Right to opt out of AI features

**Check**: Users should be able to choose not to use the AI feature or have their data processed by it (especially when the feature isn't essential to the service).

**Look for**: Settings for disabling AI features (e.g. "Don't use my messages for AI suggestions").

**Severity**: Medium when AI is non-essential and no opt-out exists.

**Fix recommendation**: Add an opt-out per AI feature. Honor it on the server side (don't just hide the UI).

---

## 16. Watermarking and provenance for generated media

**Check**: EU AI Act Art. 50 will require synthetic media labeling. C2PA / SynthID and similar standards are emerging.

**Look for**: Image/video/audio generation features. Whether output carries a provenance signal.

**Severity**: Medium in EU scope.

**Fix recommendation**: Apply provider-supported watermarking (SynthID, etc.). Embed C2PA manifests where the provider supports it.
